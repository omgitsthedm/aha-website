# After Hours Agenda — Brand Kit Needs

Status: Creative brief and delivery checklist
Current identity: **The name `After Hours Agenda` only**
Not approved: any previous logo, sheep, icon, monogram, lockup, slogan, color, type treatment, illustration, pattern, texture, campaign system, or `AHA` abbreviation.

## Purpose

Create a complete, original, legally usable identity system for After Hours Agenda that works across ecommerce, apparel, packaging, email, social media, photography, print, events, and future collections.

The finished kit must provide a coherent system, not a folder of unrelated logo exports. It must be distinctive at a glance, usable by non-designers, accessible on the web, economical to reproduce, and flexible enough to support many product collections without redesigning the company every season.

## 1. Decisions required before design begins

The creative team must resolve these questions in a short written strategy before drawing marks:

### Business and audience

- What does After Hours Agenda sell today?
- What may it sell three years from now?
- Who is the primary customer by behavior, not demographics alone?
- What does that customer already buy, read, follow, attend, or care about?
- Why should they choose this company instead of another graphic-apparel store?
- What price and quality expectations must the identity support?
- Is the company primarily a fashion label, graphic publisher, cultural project, product studio, or a deliberate hybrid?
- Which markets matter first: local New York, US direct-to-consumer, wholesale, international, events, or collaborations?

### Meaning

- What does `After Hours Agenda` mean in one plain sentence?
- What is the company for?
- What is it against?
- What promise can it reliably keep?
- Which parts of the founder story are public, and which remain private?
- What emotional response should the identity create?
- Which three adjectives should describe the brand?
- Which three adjectives must never describe it?

### Competitive position

- Identify 8-12 direct and adjacent competitors.
- Record what each competitor visually owns: colors, symbols, typography, photography, voice, and retail behavior.
- Identify category clichés to avoid.
- Define the visual and verbal territory After Hours Agenda can credibly own.
- Test whether the proposed identity is recognizable when competitor names are hidden.

### Name usage

- Confirm the exact legal and public spelling: `After Hours Agenda`.
- Decide whether `AHA` is allowed as a public abbreviation. It is not approved by default.
- Decide whether the name appears in title case, uppercase, or a custom wordmark construction.
- Define possessive, shortened, social-handle, URL, and partnership usage.
- Define whether any descriptor accompanies the name and where it may appear.

## 4. Core identity deliverables

### Primary wordmark

- Custom or deliberately typeset `After Hours Agenda`
- Horizontal master
- Stacked master if compositionally justified
- One-color black
- One-color white
- Approved color version
- Small-size version if the primary construction loses detail
- Vector source with outlined-text export
- Spacing and minimum-size specification
- Examples on light, dark, photographic, and product-colored backgrounds

### Symbol or logomark

A symbol is optional. Do not create one merely because brand kits usually contain one.

If a symbol is created, it must:

- Have a documented idea connected to the strategy
- Be ownable and distinguishable from stock icons
- Work without the name only after recognition has been established
- Remain legible at 16px and in one-color embroidery
- Avoid unlicensed source material
- Avoid mascots, initials, clocks, moons, city skylines, subway symbols, and nightlife clichés unless research proves a genuinely original solution
- Include optical corrections for small sizes
- Include clear misuse guidance

### Monogram or abbreviation

- Create only if `AHA` is formally approved
- Test for conflicts with existing organizations and common meanings
- Do not place `AHA` and `After Hours Agenda` in the same lockup unless the final guidelines explicitly define a justified exception
- Provide a standalone monogram only when it serves a real application

### Lockups

Provide only the lockups that real surfaces require:

- Primary horizontal
- Compact horizontal
- Stacked
- Sponsor/partnership row
- Wordmark-only
- Symbol-only, if approved
- Collection/collaboration endorsement lockup

For every lockup define fixed proportions, spacing, alignment, minimum size, and approved colorways.

## 5. Color system

Deliver a production-tested palette, not isolated swatches.

Required:

- Primary brand color or intentionally monochrome system
- Secondary colors only when they have defined jobs
- Neutral scale
- Functional success, warning, error, and information colors kept separate from identity colors
- Light and dark surface assignments
- Text and icon color assignments
- Hover, focus, active, selected, disabled, and visited states
- Product-photography background guidance
- Print equivalents: Pantone where needed, CMYK, RGB, HEX, and spot-color notes
- Fabric, embroidery, screen-print, DTG, DTF, sublimation, and heat-transfer reproduction tests
- WCAG 2.2 AA contrast matrix for website combinations
- Guidance for color-blind-safe state communication

Do not approve a color because it looks good on one calibrated screen. Test it on phones, laptop displays, paper, cotton, embroidery thread, and low-cost packaging.


Do not use `final`, `final2`, `latest`, dates as the only version identifier, or filenames that claim an orientation the art does not match.

## 17. Legal and ownership requirements

- Confirm company ownership of all final work in writing
- Record designer and contributor agreements
- Document font, stock, photo, model, illustration, and texture licenses
- Run trademark clearance on the name, symbol, monogram, and tagline
- Run visual similarity checks against relevant fashion marks
- Confirm domain and social-handle ownership
- Store releases and contracts in the approved business system
- Do not use generative output as a final mark without provenance review, originality review, and human vector reconstruction
- Do not incorporate found artwork, subway symbols, municipal marks, album art, or unlicensed cultural material

## 18. Acceptance tests

The kit is not complete until it passes all of these:

### Recognition

- Distinct from direct competitors when shown without the name
- Recognizable in one color
- Legible at required small sizes
- Does not depend on explanation to look intentional

### Production

- Reproduces in embroidery and one-color printing
- Works on black, white, and common garment colors
- Survives low-cost packaging and office printing
- Includes vendor-ready files that open without missing fonts

### Digital

- Favicon is recognizable at 16px
- Navigation lockup works at 320px
- Social avatar remains legible when circularly cropped
- Open Graph card reads on mobile
- Website color pairs meet WCAG 2.2 AA
- Focus and state styling remain visible
- Asset weight is appropriate for web performance

### Governance

- One canonical source exists for every approved asset
- Every export maps to the asset manifest
- Deprecated assets are clearly marked and removed from active folders
- No contradictory direction, color, or typography rules exist
- A non-designer can choose the correct asset without guessing

## Final delivery standard

The finished brand kit must make these questions effortless:

1. Which asset do I use here?
2. What size and color should it be?
3. What can I say in this context?
4. Which image belongs here?
5. Can a vendor reproduce it correctly?
6. Can a customer read and use it accessibly?
7. Who owns it and where is the source?
8. What is approved, deprecated, or still missing?

If the kit cannot answer those questions without guesswork, it is not finished.
